Who is [Diodore of Tarsus](https://gedsh.bethmardutho.org/Diodore-of-Tarsus) (c. 330-390)?

[Diodorus Tarsensis](www.biblindex.org/en/authors/diodorus-tarsensis)

The first exponent of the School of Antioch (qv) after Lucian of Antioch (qv)

One of the three Greek Doctors with Theodore and Nestorius in the Church of the East (qv)

References to his works are found in Dadisho Qatraya (qv) and Isaac of Nineveh (qv).

Student of Eusebius of Emesa (qv).

Teacher of Theodore of Mopsuestia, John Chrysostom, Theodoret and Severian of Gabala

For a time, he ran an askētērion (Socrates, Ecclesiastical History 6.3, PG 67, Sozomen, Ecclesiastical History 8.2), where he initiated young men into the pursuit of perfection. Among them were Theodore of Mopsuestia and John Chrysostom. The latter became his favorite disciple and retained a grateful admiration for his former master, which he expressed in a public eulogy in which he compared him to John the Baptist (qv).

Chrysostom - Laus Diodori (PG 52.761-766 = CPG 4406)

Come, let us show how this wise father of ours also emulated John’s manner of life. For that man had neither table nor bed nor house upon earth, but this man never had such either. And we are witnesses of this, how the whole time he continued demonstrating an apostolic way of life, having nothing his own, but being supported by others, and devoting himself to prayer and teaching of the word. That man preached beyond the river while living in the wilderness and this man once took the whole city beyond the river and provided sound instruction. That man resided in prison and was beheaded because of his frankness for the sake of the law, but this man, too, was often expelled from his homeland because of his frankness for the sake of the faith. And he was often beheaded for this same reason as well, if not in fact, certainly by intent. For the enemies of the truth, unable to bear the power of his tongue, contrived countless plots from every side, and the Lord delivered him from all of these. Come therefore, let us hear this tongue on account of which he was both endangered and saved. About which someone would not sin by saying that which Moses said about the Promised Land. What did he say about it? It was flowing with milk and honey (Exodus 3:8). Therefore so that we may both enjoy milk and be satiated with honey, here we bring our word to a close so we can hear the lyre and the trumpet. For whenever Ι have the pleasure of his words in mind, I call this man’s voice a lyre; but whenever the power of his thoughts, some warlike trumpet, of the kind which the Jews had at the time when they brought down the walls of Jericho. For just as then, when the sound of the trumpets struck more violently than stones and consumed and destroyed everything, so too the voice of this one now is not weaker than that trumpet, and attacks the heretics’ fortresses and tears down arguments and every arrogance which puts on airs against the knowledge of God. But so that you may learn these things not through our tongue, but through that of this man, let us conclude our word, sending up glory to God who produces such teachers, because to Him be the glory unto the ages of ages. Amen.

Under the reign of Constantius and even more so under Valens, Flavian and Diodorus were the most influential and determined leaders of the Catholics of Antioch. Although they were simply laymen, they adopted the practice of gathering the faithful to pray in the chapels of the martyrs, built on the outskirts of the city, hence the name campenses given to them by Jerome (Ep. 15, PL 22), and leading them in the singing of the Psalms according to the then-new antiphonal form (Theodoret, Ecclesiastical History 2.19, PG 82; Cavallera, pp. 53-54).

_Diodorus wrote, drawing streams of teaching from the Divine source, being a conductor of the Spirit._ (Theodoret).

Over a century after his death in 499, he was condemned in Constantinople as a precursor of Nestorius and his many writings were destroyed. Only his Commentary on Psalms has survived.
